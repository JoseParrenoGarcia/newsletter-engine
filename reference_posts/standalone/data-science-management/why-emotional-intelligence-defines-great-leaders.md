# Why emotional intelligence defines great leaders

*A breakdown of Goleman's framework — the hidden factor behind leadership success.*

---

This week, a mentee asked me a question that caught me off guard:

*"What's the main trait that separates bad, good, and great leaders?"*

I gave an answer (decent, but not great). The question stuck with me. Because if there is one thing that shapes careers, companies, and teams, it's leadership. And yet, we rarely stop to ask: **what really makes a leader effective?**

Before diving in, let me ask you a question. Think about the worst manager you have ever had. Now, think about the best. Were they the most technically brilliant person in the room? Did they have all the right credentials? Or was it something else that made them stand out?

I once refused to be managed by one of the smartest people I have ever met. They just… lacked something else. Which brings us to the real question: **Why do some leaders thrive while others — who seem equally or even more qualified — fail?**

Thankfully we have Daniel Goleman to help us. In 1998, Goleman published *What Makes a Leader*, an HBR article that changed how we understand leadership. Based on studies across nearly 200 companies, Goleman found that IQ and technical skills matter — but they don't set great leaders apart. **Emotional Intelligence does.**

In this post, I will break down the key takeaways from Goleman's work, so you can absorb them in 5 minutes (instead of reading through all his research!).

## The hidden factor behind leadership success

For decades, leadership was seen as a mix of IQ, technical skills, and hard work. The best engineers became CTOs. The top sales performers became VPs. The sharpest analysts became executives.

But history tells a different story. Think about the great conquerors of the ancient world: Napoleon, Caesar, Alexander the Great. They inspired loyalty, read their enemies, and built influence beyond the battlefield. There must have been other traits they displayed which helped them achieve the feats they did.

Even better examples are those who were not conquerors: **Lincoln** (with his team of rivals leadership approach), **Mandela** (focused on reconciliation instead of revenge), and **Gandhi** (leading an entire nation to independence using nonviolent resistance). These are figures who must have had an incredible grasp of emotional intelligence to lead nations under turmoil without reverting to brute force.

Of course, leadership context has changed, but one thing hasn't: it's never been just about making decisions and giving orders. In today's world, leaders need to:

- Navigate uncertainty without panicking
- Build trust in remote and hybrid teams
- Motivate employees who have more options than ever before
- Resolve conflicts without creating resentment
- Influence without authority, as teams become more cross-functional and decentralised

## The myth of the "born leader" (and why it's holding you back)

Think about the phrase "natural-born leader." It's the idea that leadership is something you either have, or you don't. That charisma, confidence, and decision-making are pre-installed — an elite trait only a few are lucky enough to inherit.

I can see why people believe it. Some people seem to command attention effortlessly.

**But what if we are wrong? What if leadership is something that anyone can develop?**

Goleman addresses this directly:

> *"Are people born with certain levels of empathy, for example, or do they acquire empathy as a result of life's experiences? The answer is both. Scientific inquiry strongly suggests that there is a genetic component to emotional intelligence. Psychological and developmental research indicates that nurture plays a role as well. How much of each perhaps will never be known, but research and practice clearly demonstrate that emotional intelligence can be learned."*

In other words, EQ enhances over time. The question isn't *if* EQ can be learned, but *how much*.

### But EQ cannot be learned through theoretical courses

So, if EQ can be learned, then why aren't more people great at it? Why do so many leadership training programs fail?

Goleman explains:

> *"Emotional intelligence is born largely in the neurotransmitters of the brain's limbic system, which governs feelings, impulses, and drives. Research indicates that the limbic system learns best through motivation, extended practice, and feedback."*

Unlike technical skills, which can be learned in a classroom, EQ requires something deeper: **motivation, extended practice, and real-time feedback.**

## The 5 key skills that set high-EQ leaders apart

### 1. Self-awareness: the foundation of emotional intelligence

Self-awareness is the ability to recognise and understand your emotions, strengths, weaknesses, and how they impact others.

A self-aware leader is recognised by their self-confidence:

> *"They have a firm grasp of their capabilities and are less likely to set themselves up to fail by, for example, overstretching on assignments. They know, too, when to ask for help. And the risks they take on the job are calculated. They won't ask for a challenge that they know they can't handle alone."*

They can also admit mistakes instead of blaming others:

> *"Self-aware people know, and are comfortable talking about, their limitations and strengths, and they often demonstrate a thirst for constructive criticism."*

### 2. Self-regulation: keeping emotions in check

If self-awareness is recognising emotions, self-regulation is managing them effectively. Goleman describes self-regulation as *"an ongoing inner conversation"* that prevents people from becoming prisoners of their feelings.

A self-regulated leader:

- **Doesn't react impulsively to bad news:** *"People who are in control of their feelings and impulses — that is, people who are reasonable — are able to create an environment of trust and fairness. And self-regulation has a trickle-down effect. No one wants to be known as a hothead when the boss is known for her calm approach."*
- **Adapts to change instead of resisting it:** *"When a new program is announced, they don't panic; instead, they are able to suspend judgment, seek out information, and listen to the executives as they explain the new program. As the initiative moves forward, these people are able to move with it."*

### 3. Motivation: the drive to achieve beyond external rewards

High-EQ leaders aren't just motivated by money or status. They tend to be driven by a deep internal desire to achieve and improve.

A highly motivated leader sets high standards for themselves and their teams:

> *"Those with leadership potential are motivated by a deeply embedded desire to achieve for the sake of achievement."*

Their drive to surpass goals and keep score can be contagious. And crucially, they see failures as learning opportunities rather than personal defeats:

> *"People with high motivation remain optimistic even when the score is against them. In such cases, self-regulation combines with achievement motivation to overcome the frustration and depression that come after a setback or failure."*

### 4. Empathy: understanding and connecting with others

Empathy is the ability to understand and share the feelings of others. It is the most easily recognised EQ component — and one of the most underrated leadership skills.

> *"When it comes to business, we rarely hear people praised, let alone rewarded, for their empathy."*

Yet empathy is precisely what enables a leader to read a room, retain talent, and make people feel seen.

### 5. Social skill: influence over authority

Social skill is about managing relationships, building networks, and leading with influence rather than authority:

> *"It's not just a matter of friendliness — rather, it's friendliness with a purpose: moving people in the direction you desire."*

A leader with strong social skills:

> *"Social skill is the culmination of the other dimensions of emotional intelligence. Socially skilled people are adept at managing teams — that's their empathy at work. Likewise, they are expert persuaders — a manifestation of self-awareness, self-regulation, and empathy combined."*

And social skills is not small talk:

> *"Socially skilled people may at times appear not to be working while at work. They seem to be idly schmoozing — chatting in the hallways with colleagues or joking around with people who are not even connected to their 'real' jobs. They build bonds widely because they know that in these fluid times, they may need help someday from people they are just getting to know today."*

## The 5 together

These five EQ skills don't operate in isolation. They reinforce each other.

- A leader who is self-aware is better at self-regulation
- A leader with empathy is better at handling difficult conversations
- A leader with strong motivation can inspire others to stay focused in tough times

**The best part? Every single one of these skills can be developed.**

---

## Why emotional intelligence matters more than ever

We live in a world where leadership is becoming less about authority and more about influence.

- Remote and hybrid teams require leaders who can build trust from a distance
- Cross-functional teams mean that "command-and-control" leadership doesn't work anymore
- AI and automation are taking over technical tasks, but EQ remains uniquely human

And still, emotional intelligence is treated as a "soft skill" rather than the leadership superpower it actually is. After reading Goleman's research, you know there is nothing soft about it.

---

## How do you actually build EQ?

EQ isn't learned through books or lectures. Here are 3 simple ways to start improving it today:

**✅ Practice self-awareness daily.** Take five minutes at the end of each day to reflect: What triggered me today? How did I react? Did my emotions help or hurt my leadership?

**✅ Ask for real feedback (not just surface-level praise).** Most leaders don't realise how they actually come across. Instead of "How did I do?" ask: *"What's one thing I could have done better in that meeting?"*

**✅ Get comfortable with discomfort.** If a difficult conversation, tough feedback, or a high-pressure situation makes you uneasy, lean into it. Emotional intelligence grows in moments of discomfort.

---

## Final thought: the leaders who master EQ win the long game

Technical skills and intelligence will get you in the door. **Emotional intelligence will determine how far you go.**

The best leaders aren't necessarily the smartest people in the room. They are the ones who understand people, build trust, and create environments where others can do their best work.

That is the kind of leadership worth aspiring to.
