# Are meetings taking over your calendar? Here is how I fixed mine.

*I restructured my week around the 3Ps with one clear goal: keeping 50% of my calendar open.*

---

This week, I have been reflecting on the changes I made to my weekly schedule once I got to leading multiple teams. The way I managed my time as a manager of a single small squad didn't scale to bigger teams and way more projects.

This is why I wanted to share with you the changes that worked for me, in case they could also work for you.

Let's dive in!

---

## The IC to manager time shift

Are you a newly promoted manager? Or, if you already are a manager, do you remember your transition from an individual contributor role?

As of the time of writing, I have been leading data science teams for the past 4 to 5 years, but I still remember one of my main struggles at the beginning of my journey as a manager: **time management**.

Data Scientists or Senior Data Scientists tend to focus on 1 or 2 problem spaces. The IC role provides you the space to deliver on a deep problem, removing noise from other projects and admin work. You will probably have some ad-hoc chats with engineering colleagues or other data science peers, but these are necessary because technical designs and system dependencies are important for delivery. One weekly stakeholder meeting, maybe. **As an IC, your week is around 20% meetings with 80% technical work.**

Now… what would happen if, instead of working on 1 project, you oversee 3 to 5 projects and 6 to 12 direct reports? If you are not careful with time management, the hourly balance can balloon to **80% meeting time with only 20% of time to focus on actual project details**.

In my opinion, this is not healthy. If you find yourself in this situation, I hope this article can show you a different way of organising your week.

---

## A challenging personal experience: leading 2 Data Science squads

Due to a set of circumstances, in January 2024 I found myself leading 2 data science squads. I had been leading the marketing DS team for a couple of years, and senior leadership asked me to steer the hotels team. In total, with both teams, I was overseeing **10 individual contributors and 5 projects**. I knew that time was going to be a problem, and I was right.

[Visual: screenshot | author-generated | context illustration — Jose's overloaded calendar from January 2024, showing less than 30% open slots]

Clearly, I was not complying with what I mentioned in the introduction. My open calendar had less than 30% of the weekly allocation. I had too many direct reports, too many projects, too many deep dives and too many squad ceremonies. We also had a seniority problem in the team, which meant that delegation was complicated — therefore it fell on me to attend pretty much all sessions. It wasn't a bad time management problem. **It was a being too thinly spread out.**

Nevertheless, this highlights what I said: this is not healthy. I couldn't sustain that level of detailed work, nor was I offering the best of me to my direct reports. I also got feedback that there was no space in my calendar for some of these projects.

The solution, in the short term, was to assign a dedicated lead to the hotels team. This meant the volume of 1-on-1s, ceremonies and project deep dives would be significantly smaller for me, creating open space in the agenda.

> **If you find yourself with barely 20 or 30% of your calendar open for unscheduled meetings or deep work, it is time to think how to solve it.**

---

## The 50:50 goal

As a data scientist, I need a metric of success to measure and target. In this case, I came up with a **50:50 split between scheduled meetings and open slots**.

Scheduled meetings are the irreplaceable ones — the time investments you need to make to ensure your teams are working smoothly and project delivery is on track. Because they are irreplaceable, you shouldn't have more than 50% of your agenda committed to them. You will not have flexibility or capacity otherwise.

For example:
- When would I have time to do interviews?
- What if there was a meeting to discuss urgent problems?
- What if I needed to extend my 1-on-1s to talk about something important?
- What if I need to travel for work?

I can guarantee you that the open slots tend to get filled if you are in a senior manager position. The goal is to make sure you have the flexibility to attend unexpected needs or move 1-on-1s around if necessary.

## What are the 3Ps?

Up until now, we have covered the issue of not having enough open slots for flexibility. Now, let's focus on the irreplaceable meetings. If you can only invest up to 50% of your week, what are the most important things to focus on?

For me, these are:
1. **People**
2. **Projects**
3. **Process**

(And in that order of importance.)

[Visual: diagram | Napkin.AI | framework illustration — the 3Ps pyramid showing People > Projects > Process]

---

## People (~20% of the week)

The whole point of being a manager is to **empower your team**. And you can only do that by spending time with people, knowing them, and helping them.

### 1-on-1s with ICs (30 minutes)

I want to spend my 1-on-1s knowing what the other person did on the weekend, if they are feeling excited or frustrated, and then covering the most important project topics for the week. **These sessions are for the individual, not for me.**

Why 30 minutes and not 1 hour? I have tried many variations. Ideally, I would love to dedicate 1 hour to every direct report. The reality is that this would eat into my calendar and leave little flexibility. By constraining to 30 minutes, both the IC and I have to focus on the most important thing for the week. Sure, many weeks we run over — that's fine. But the default is 30 minutes.

### 1-on-1 with the manager I oversee (1 hour)

This is longer because I am accountable not just for my direct ICs but also for the other team's delivery. As I am not in the day-to-day of the other team, we need dedicated time to cover: why are they deciding to focus on that feature? How is it tied to the quarterly plan? Has the team communicated the results more widely? Is the team missing tooling?

### 1-on-1 with my own manager (1 hour)

My manager helps me navigate the wider organisation, escalate blockers, and understand the strategic direction. This time is essential.

### Optional skip-level 1-on-1s (~1 hour)

I have ~1 hour blocked for informal chats with ICs from other teams. Not a formal 1-on-1 — they have their manager for that. It's an open chat. These skip-level or non-direct-report conversations give me a pulse on how people are feeling without creating reporting overhead.

**In total: ~15–20% of the weekly time devoted to people.**

## Projects (~15% of the week)

As a senior data science manager, delivery is as important as the individual folks in the team. We need to deliver value. And you can't deliver value if you don't understand what is happening.

### Deep dives (the fun part)

Deep dives are the most enjoyable meetings of the week. The IC(s) working on a project and I get together for 1 hour to cover: delivery against plan, new ideas, brainstorms, and peer reviews. We look at mini-milestones, check progress, adjust if necessary, and look ahead to what needs to happen today to enable work planned 2 or 3 sprints ahead.

Once the "admin" side of things is covered, we go into the **"fun zone"**: peer reviewing the XGBoost being developed, suggesting metrics to better communicate data quality, or looking at execution times and ETL orchestration. In other words, the geeky side of the job.

### Performance meetings with stakeholders

Weekly catchups with stakeholders are essential. In our projects, stakeholders tend to be a mix of product managers, marketers, and engineers. There is always an agenda:

1. 5–10 minute progress report
2. Technical discussions (e.g. we present analysis from our bidding algorithm, stakeholders ask about possible scenarios — these enquiries shape future work)
3. ~10 minutes for accountability: who is doing what and by when? What are the next 2 items to focus on? Who needs to unblock what?

*Note: I try to skip these when the IC is comfortable and has enough context. If you provide the IC with a space to own, they thrive. If you are there, by default, eyes turn to you for Data Science input. Whilst my agenda has ~3 hours blocked for this, I probably use only 1 hour per week.*

### 1-on-1s with senior leadership

One thing I have discovered that unblocks delivery: **having a close relationship with senior leadership**. Try your best to get 30 minutes every 2 weeks with those who call the shots. I know they are busy, but they actually look forward to getting insights on what is happening and how they can help. The list of issues that senior leadership has unblocked for me is long — and that is because of these regular meetings. They are 3 to 4 levels above me… so what?

**In total: ~10–15% of the weekly time devoted to projects.**

*(I can assure you that now everyone in our meetings wears hoodies.)*

## Process (~15% of the week)

Process means the ceremonies not directly tied to any specific project or person, but necessary to ensure we don't lose sight of the bigger picture.

### Squad ceremonies

- **Retros:** As a senior DS manager, I attend my direct squad's retros and also the retros of the other squad, to stay connected to what's happening.
- **Squad sharing sessions:** A free space to brainstorm, showcase work, and learn from each other. This is one of the meetings the squad really enjoys. It's geeky, it's banter, it's fun, and we learn a truck load of things from each other.

### Discipline ceremonies

- **All-hands (every 2 weeks):** 2 presenters share their latest work, plus our Director of DS shares a company-wide download. Helps everyone understand what other squads are doing — sometimes there is overlap in technical solutions, tooling, or data issues.
- **Ranking guild:** ICs from different ranking projects (hotels, flights, car hire) get together to discuss ideas and reading papers. Not in the main calendar screenshot but it happens.

### Discipline management

With my manager peers, we meet weekly to discuss the data science discipline more widely: hiring needs, interview formats, people performance, internal rotations, production standards. And the list goes on.

**In total: ~12–15% of the weekly time devoted to process.**

## So 50% of your week is "open"…

That's your starting point on a weekly basis. You check on Sunday how the week looks and think: "hey, I can do X, Y and Z on Wednesday and Friday."

Well, there is one thing I can guarantee you:

> **A free slot in the calendar does not mean focus time for you.**

What does my 50% open week really look like?

1. Extra ad-hoc 1-on-1s
2. Sudden issues which need alignment with other leads
3. Hiring *(when hiring kicks in, you can forget about your open slots)*
4. Kids. Yes, kids. It's Murphy's law: if you have saved a day for deep focused work, your kids will make sure that is not the case.
5. Company town halls

*(My recommendation: if you want to have a deep focus slot, block it in your calendar with a fancy meeting name.)*

---

## Summary

If you find yourself in a managerial position with very little "free" time in your calendar, review your priorities. Try to ensure that you have, at least, **50% of your week "free"** to cover unexpected needs.

The 3Ps give you a structure for the other 50%:
- **People:** your most important investment — know your team, empower them, don't skip 1-on-1s
- **Projects:** deep dives and stakeholder alignment — but delegate when the IC can own it
- **Process:** ceremonies that keep the bigger picture visible — keep them lean

This is how I currently lead 1 Data Science squad of 6 data scientists, manage the lead of another 6-person squad, and am accountable for 5 to 6 projects. As of today, this time management framework has worked for me. Time will tell if, with bigger responsibilities, I need to shift this way of working.
