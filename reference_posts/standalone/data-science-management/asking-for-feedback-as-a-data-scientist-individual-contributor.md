# Asking for feedback as a Data Scientist (or Engineer) Individual Contributor

*Receive clear and useful feedback. Ditch generic questions. More than 60 example questions for you to use.*

---

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Marsha Reid) | mood setter — opening image, person in a reflective or conversational pose]

This week, I have been thinking about how tricky it can be to get useful feedback. You ask for it, hoping for something actionable, and what do you get? Vague compliments. Generic advice. Maybe a polite "keep doing what you're doing."

The problem isn't that people don't want to help. **The problem is that most don't know how to give good feedback.**

In this post, I will share a better way to ask for feedback, so you get insights that actually help. I will break down the right questions to ask, when to ask them, and give you 60+ examples you can start using today.

Let's dive in!

---

## Why does generic feedback happen?

Imagine you are in the usual cycle of a performance review. Your manager has asked you to get some feedback from peers and stakeholders. With the task in hand, you prepare the 4 questions everyone has used in the past (in fact, it's in the 'asking for feedback' template your company provides):

1. One thing you would like to see me continue trying is…
2. One thing you would like to see me develop is…
3. Something I have done really well recently is…
4. It has had a positive impact because…

Honestly, when I receive this template, it makes me want to cry. It is sooooo generalist.

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Igor Omilaev) | analogy reinforcer — visual representing the frustration of generic, unhelpful responses]

Most of the times, feedback comes back with lots of positive comments: "Keep doing what you are doing", "Great presentation", "Thanks for helping me on short notice." These are definitely nice to receive, but I personally want to grow through feedback. If you feel the same way, it's time to change what questions you ask and when you ask them.

---

## Why do I provide feedback and how do I provide it?

There are 3 things I want to achieve when providing feedback to someone else:

1. **Recognise** something that an individual is doing well.
2. **Develop** an individual to perform more efficiently / effectively or learn something new for their growth.
3. **Improve or correct** behaviours which negatively impact the individual's performance.

At work, to make sure that I am fair and consistent, I lean on the **SBI framework** — Situation, Behaviour, Impact and Next steps.

The key aspect of SBI is about being **specific**. For example, instead of me telling my colleague "you did a great presentation", I would say: *"Your presentation was great because you were constantly reminding the main takeaway in each slide and your charts had very little clutter. This made it very clear what we would be doing next."*

If the key of providing good feedback is to be specific, then the same applies to **asking** for feedback.

## Breaking down feedback dimensions into categories

My suggestion is to think in sub-categories that fit each of the 3 core dimensions: Recognition, Development, and Correction.

## Questions related to recognition

Recognition feedback tells you what you are doing well and should continue. I break it into three sub-dimensions: **Delivery**, **Teamwork**, and **Communication**.

### Delivery

- Was there a specific work stream that achieved or surpassed expected performance?
- Was there a specific contribution that had a positive impact on the team?
- Were there any innovative ideas or approaches I've brought forward that led to favourable outcomes?
- Were there any challenges I navigated particularly well that improved the project's outcome?
- Was there a situation where my data-driven insights and analysis contributed to a key decision?
- Did I effectively communicate complex data findings in a way that influenced stakeholder decisions?

### Teamwork

- How well do I contribute to our team collaboration sessions?
- Was there a specific situation where my support helped you?
- Was there a moment where I helped foster collaboration within the team to overcome a major challenge?
- How well do I facilitate communication between team members, particularly when dealing with cross-functional teams?
- Have I contributed to the team's morale or culture in a noticeable way?
- Was there an occasion where I exhibited leadership qualities, even if not in a leadership role?
- Have I stepped up in a crisis or deadline situation to provide clear direction or reduce friction?
- Is there an instance where I helped coach or mentor a junior team member or peer?
- Have I demonstrated accountability by owning outcomes, even when facing difficult situations?

### Communication

Since data scientists often have to present complex findings to non-technical stakeholders, communication is a critical skill. If you get recognition in this area, you will stand out amongst your peers.

- How effectively do I communicate complex data insights to non-technical audiences?
- Was there a moment where my communication helped bridge a gap between technical and non-technical team members?
- Have I improved my storytelling abilities in presenting data to make outcomes more compelling?
- Was my feedback during meetings clear and constructive? Did it guide discussions toward actionable steps?

> **Tip:** You can tailor these questions by being even more specific. Ask about a particular presentation you gave, an experiment that was run, or a specific meeting you led.

## Questions related to development

Recognition covered what you have done right. But what about things that could have been better? The best pieces of feedback are the ones that allow you to grow. I propose 4 dimensions: **Technical Skills**, **Personal Skills**, **Problem Solving**, and **Vision & Strategic Thinking**.

### Technical skillset improvement

- Are there any best practices when putting ML in production that I should be doing?
- Is there any algorithm framework that could help me with this type of problem?
- Is my code structure easy to maintain and understand for other team members?
- Are there specific data engineering practices I could implement to improve the efficiency of my pipelines?
- Have I demonstrated strong model evaluation techniques, or is there a better approach I could use (e.g. cross-validation, hyperparameter tuning)?
- Can you suggest ways I could improve the interpretability of my models?

### Personal skillset developments

- Is there any feedback you have for how I handled a difficult conversation or situation?
- Are there any tips or examples where I could improve my leadership skills?
- What would you suggest for me to change in order to gain more influence with other teams?
- Was there a situation where I could have been more assertive in defending my technical choices?
- What could I do to improve my presentation skills when communicating insights to non-technical stakeholders?
- Are there any habits or practices I could adopt to improve my time management or organisation skills?

### Problem-solving

- Can you provide feedback on my approach to problem-solving, particularly how I frame problems and consider various solutions?
- Are there any alternative methods or models I should explore to improve the accuracy or efficiency of my solutions?
- How could I improve my ability to identify the root cause of a problem and develop a clear action plan?
- Do you have any suggestions for improving my hypothesis-driven approach when testing an idea?

### Vision & strategic thinking

- Do you think I sufficiently considered the long-term implications of my decisions in recent projects?
- Were there moments where I could have better connected my work to the broader business objectives?
- How could I improve my ability to anticipate and communicate potential risks in our projects?

## Questions related to correction

Correction feedback is about identifying specific issues and finding ways to address them. Three key sub-dimensions: **Time Management**, **Feedback Integration**, and **Attention to Detail**.

### Time management

- I didn't meet the deadline last time. What could I have done differently in terms of planning and organising my workload?
- Was there something I could have prioritised differently to meet the deadline?
- Could you provide feedback on how I manage competing priorities across multiple projects?
- Are there tools or methodologies (e.g. agile frameworks, time-blocking) you would recommend for better time management?
- How can I improve my ability to estimate the time required for complex data science tasks, like feature engineering or model training?

### Feedback integration

- Have there been instances where I didn't fully incorporate previous feedback? How could I have done so more effectively?
- Are there any specific areas where I've struggled to adopt feedback, and how can I improve my approach?
- Do you feel that I've responded well to constructive criticism, or are there instances where I became defensive?

### Attention to detail

- Can you identify areas where my attention to detail was lacking, and how I could avoid similar mistakes in the future?
- How could I improve the way I validate data or results to ensure they are error-free before delivering them to stakeholders?
- Were there moments where I should have performed more thorough checks (e.g. data validation, peer reviews) before finalising my work?
- Are there habits or practices you could recommend to help me catch errors earlier in the workflow?

## The magic question

We have covered loads of specific example questions across 3 feedback areas. But there is **one magic question** that you can always use to trigger great responses.

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Frugal Flyer) | section separator — transition to the magic question section]

> **In situation [X], what would you have done differently?**

Whenever you ask this question, the following happens:

1. The other person puts themselves in your shoes
2. They need to really think about what was done vs what they expected
3. Solving a 'challenge' is mentally attractive, and many people love to provide solutions

The replies you get back tend to be detailed. You will probably learn solutions you hadn't thought about. You will understand how the other person thinks about your work. And if you actually apply these suggestions and praise the other person for the ideas, your influence with them will improve.

## How can you increase the frequency of feedback?

Ideally feedback should be continuous, but nearly everyone falls into the trap of asking for it twice a year (during performance reviews). One thing that really helps with specificity is **frequency**.

Try the following:

1. **After delivering a presentation** — ask one or two people for specific feedback on your delivery or content immediately after
2. **After closing a project or sprint** — ask your team lead or a stakeholder for a short retrospective on your contribution
3. **After a difficult conversation** — ask a trusted peer how they thought you handled it
4. **After implementing someone's previous feedback** — close the loop by asking if they've noticed a change

The more of these informal check-ins you do, the more feedback you get. And the more regular feedback you get, the sooner you can act on it.

## Summary

By shifting away from generic feedback questions and focusing on targeted areas such as recognition, development, and correction, you can foster more meaningful conversations that drive real improvements. Frequent, specific feedback helps you continuously refine your skills, gain valuable insights, and strengthen your relationships with peers and stakeholders.

**If there is a main takeaway it would be:**

> *If the key of providing good feedback is to be specific, then the same applies to asking for feedback.*
