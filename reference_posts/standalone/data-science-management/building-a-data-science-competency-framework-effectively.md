# Data Science competency framework: how to define roles and growth

*How to structure career progression in Data Science with a competency framework.*

---

[Visual: illustration | Dall-E | mood setter — opening concept image for competency frameworks and career progression]

This week I have been reflecting on how important a competency framework is to define clear career paths and improve role clarity. Without it, it is actually really difficult to have meaningful progression conversations, constructive end-of-year feedback, and calibrate fairly across a wider group of people.

In this post, I will walk you through the Data Science competency framework we use at Skyscanner. However, this post will also give you the template to build one for your own team. I share Monzo's engineering framework and Intercom's product owner framework as reference points too.

Let's break it down step by step!

---

## Why do we need a competency framework?

In 2021, 365 DataScience carried out a study of 1,000s of LinkedIn profiles to understand trends in the data science discipline. A couple of points stood out: *"Very few individuals (less than 2%) have stayed at the same job for more than 5 years"* and *"The median time spent on the job by a data scientist in our study was 1.7 years."* [1]

Fortunately, I haven't seen this turnover in my teams. But I know many data scientists, and most argue that **'lack of role clarity'** is one of their top 3 challenges.

A competency framework is a means by which teams communicate which behaviours are required, valued, recognised and rewarded with respect to specific roles or levels. For example, the expectations for a junior data scientist should be different to a principal data scientist. The same applies for two similar senior data scientists, where one is an individual contributor and another is a manager.

If you are leading a data science team, I can guarantee you will get questions such as:
- *"How do I get to the next level?"*
- *"What do I need to do to get a higher bonus?"*
- *"I did everything I was told to do, why am I not getting rewarded for it?"*

You must be able to answer these in a fair and robust way. You can't be saying one thing in Q1 and something different in Q3. What worked for one direct report has to work for the rest. The vertical and horizontal roles need to be defined and transparent to everyone. In summary: you need a solid competency framework.

---

## Step 1: Defining clear Data Science roles for growth

At Skyscanner we have **5 hierarchy levels** in our Data Science discipline. In reality, we rarely hire Jr. Data Scientists, so our seniority mix starts from Data Scientist and above. It is also interesting to note that people management roles start at a certain seniority level — it is rare to see a jump from a Data Scientist IC role directly to a DS manager role.

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework illustration — career ladder showing Junior DS → DS → Senior DS → Principal DS, with parallel manager track starting at Senior DS]

The roles:
- **Junior Data Scientist** — learning, supervised, task-scoped
- **Data Scientist** — independent, project-scoped
- **Senior Data Scientist** — squad-level influence, goal-accountable
- **Principal Data Scientist** — discipline-wide influence, strategy
- **Manager track** — DS Manager → Senior DS Manager → Director of DS (parallel to IC track, starts at Senior DS level)

---

## Step 2: The eagle-eyed view — what are you looking for?

To build a strong data science competency framework, we need to define key skills and growth criteria. This exercise requires an eagle-eyed view: distilling the main themes that can describe both the day-to-day work of a data scientist and their possible future growth. Each area is later broken down into more specific requirements, but first, we need the **"foundation pillars."**

### Taking inspiration from other disciplines

I purposely look at engineering and product management frameworks, since these are the teams data scientists work with most.

**Monzo** establishes 4 core competencies: Scope, Impact, Technical skills and Behaviours. Importantly, they are not listing specific skills — it's about *"how you apply your technical skills."* Previous iterations were too detailed, which *"led to engineers check-boxing their progression, and was often overly specific to certain areas of the business."*

**Intercom** (for Product Managers) proposes 5 areas: Insight Driven, Strategy, Execution, Driving Outcomes and Leadership Behaviours. Each is broken into more focused sub-expectations — for example, Execution breaks into: start with the problem, think big start small, ship to learn, and ship the whole customer experience.

### Data Science at Skyscanner: 6 key pillars + manager track

At Skyscanner, we evaluate individuals based on **6 core competencies**:

1. **Scope** — How well defined are the problems you are tackling? What degree of leadership are you taking? How far ahead are you planning?
2. **Expertise** — Are you learning or are you a master of a skill/area? How are you ensuring others benefit from this knowledge?
3. **Delivery** — What is your degree of responsibility for each task? Think not only on your own delivery, but also how you are delivering through others.
4. **Build the right thing** — Understanding business context and translating it into value. Complexity may or may not be aligned with value.
5. **Build it right** — How robust, scalable, cost effective and understandable is your work? Are you following engineering, modelling and statistical best practices?
6. **Communication** — How effectively do you communicate with different audiences? How do you influence upwards, sideways and downwards?
7. **(Manager track)** — How do you develop your direct reports and help them grow and achieve their career goals? How do you build high-performance teams?

Think about your day-to-day work as a data scientist — anything you do can be pinned to one of these core competencies.

## Step 3: Setting clear boundaries between seniority levels

Now it's time to clearly define the expectations of each competency for different levels of seniority. Success in this step requires meeting 2 criteria:

1. **No overlap between seniority levels.** Reading the framework in ascending order of seniority should clearly show the progression jumps. Setting boundaries such as *leading 1 vs multiple projects* or *influencing the squad vs other teams* is super important.

2. **No checkbox lists.** Strike a balance between being detailed and general. Being overly specific can make your framework less adaptable over time. For example, regarding expertise, do you really want to list all possible ML solutions like time series forecasting, recommender systems, or geospatial models? That's too specific.

## The 6 competencies in detail

### 1. Scope — the art of being comfortable with uncertainty

*How well defined are the problems you are tackling? What degree of leadership are you taking?*

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework table — Scope competency across levels]

| Level | Scope | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| **Junior DS** | 📋 Task | Learning to contribute. Tackles scoped problems with little ambiguity. Requires supervision to scope and write up an initiative. Asks for help when required. |
| **Data Scientist** | 💼 Project | Contributes to a team. Tackles scoped problems. Identifies new opportunities aligned with goals with minimal support. |
| **Senior DS** | 👤 Squad | Leads or influences a team. Tackles open-ended, somewhat ambiguous problems. Influences and rallies stakeholder support. Creates scope and opportunities for others. Influences other DSs through technical direction. |
| **Principal DS** | 👤👤 Multiple squads | Leads or influences multiple teams or a deep problem space. Proactively tackles ambiguous and impactful problems. Anticipates needs, writes initiative documents, and influences at the discipline level. |

---

### 2. Expertise — mentor what you know you are good at

*Are you learning or are you a master of a skill/area? How are you ensuring others benefit from this knowledge?*

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework table — Expertise competency across levels]

| Level | Expertise | Description |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Junior DS** | 🌱 Developing | Developing discipline skills. Demonstrates necessary skills to complete assigned tasks with support. Learns new statistical/ML/Eng concepts when asked. |
| **Data Scientist** | 🌿 Competent | Solid foundational knowledge. Competent on statistical, modelling and data wrangling. Participates in knowledge sharing. Actively learns and applies best practices. |
| **Senior DS** | 🌳 Proficient / teaching | Goes beyond foundational knowledge. Serves as a resource for others. Captures team learnings accessibly. Discerns hype from utility. Drives technical culture. |
| **Principal DS** | 🌲 Master / mentoring | The go-to person for certain skills. Assumes leadership in building a knowledge-sharing culture. Raises the bar at the discipline level with quantifiable outcomes. Mentors colleagues inside and outside the discipline. |

---

### 3. Delivery — make it happen, no excuses

*What is your degree of responsibility for each task? Think not only on your own delivery, but also how you are delivering through others.*

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework table — Delivery competency across levels]

| Level | Delivery | Description |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Junior DS** | 🛠️ Contributing to tasks | Contributes to the team's timely delivery of defined business outcomes. Delivers well-scoped discrete tasks. Consistently completes work to agreed quality. |
| **Data Scientist** | 🧩 Responsible for solutions | Responsible for the timely completion of a squad goal. Capable of delivering a goal within agreed time constraints. Delivers solutions, models, and data products that are simple, scientifically sound, and scalable. |
| **Senior DS** | 🎯 Accountable for goal delivery | Accountable for the team's timely delivery of the right business outcomes. Creates well-scoped work that solves real business problems. Effectively delegates and coordinates delivery across multiple tasks. Communicates clearly with stakeholders when projects are on or off track. |
| **Principal DS** | 🚀 Accountable for multiple goals | Accountable for delivery of strategic direction across multiple squads. Creates the environment/framework to help teams with timely delivery. Enables the discipline to raise the bar on quality and speed. |

---

### 4. Build the right thing — focus on adding value

*Understanding business context and translating it into value. Complexity may or may not be aligned with value.*

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework table — Build the right thing competency across levels]

| Level | Build the right thing | Description |
|-------|----------------------|-------------|
| **Junior DS** | 📚 Learning to understand | Actively learning to understand the business impact of an analytical solution. Problem horizon: 1–2 sprints (Task). |
| **Data Scientist** | 🔍 Understand | Understands the impact of DS work and achieves business goals with the right analysis. Problem horizon: multiple sprints (Solution). Leverages data to communicate findings effectively. Proactively considers operational concerns. |
| **Senior DS** | 🤝 Partner | Partners to define the technical and feature roadmap. Problem horizon: 1–2 quarters (Goal). Identifies key stakeholders, builds strategic relationships, and course-corrects at the squad level. Understands system dependencies and PoC vs MVP stages. |
| **Principal DS** | 🌐 Influence | Influences and actively contributes to the wider company strategy. Problem horizon: multiple quarters (Strategy). Interprets company strategy to inform discipline objectives. Balances future-proofing with the need to innovate. |

---

### 5. Build it right — following best practices

*How robust, scalable, cost effective and understandable is your work?*

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework table — Build it right competency across levels]

| Level | Build it right | Description |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| **Junior DS** | 📖 Interpretable and documented | Delivers simple solutions that are interpretable and well documented. Documents work appropriately. Leaves things better than found. Relies on peers for complex deployments. |
| **Data Scientist** | 📊 Scalable and scientifically sound | Designs and delivers scalable, scientifically sound solutions. Works with peers and learns from code reviews. Iteratively improves quality in squad projects. |
| **Senior DS** | ♻️ Cost effective and reusable | Designs and delivers reusable solutions where possible. Helps peers and influences other teams to improve solution quality. Demonstrates ownership over data science health and alignment to production standards at squad level. |
| **Principal DS** | 🏆 Reference across discipline | Designs fit-for-purpose custom solutions for complex problems that are a reference across the discipline. Enables the discipline to adopt production standards. Proactively considers performance, robustness, scalability, and cost across multiple teams. |

---

### 6. Communication — connect across levels and audiences

*How effectively do you communicate with different audiences? How do you influence upwards, sideways and downwards?*

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (Datawrapper) | framework table — Communication competency across levels]

| Level | Communication | Description |
|-------|---------------|-------------|
| **Junior DS** | Basic | Communicates clearly within the team. Asks clarifying questions. Documents work for immediate colleagues. |
| **Data Scientist** | Squad | Communicates effectively within the squad. Presents findings to immediate stakeholders. Leverages data to communicate insights. |
| **Senior DS** | Cross-functional | Communicates effectively across teams. Influences stakeholders beyond the squad. Translates technical work into business value. Adapts communication style for different audiences. |
| **Principal DS** | Discipline-wide | Communicates at the discipline or company level. Crafts messaging about company-wide initiatives. Ensures teams understand reasoning and can move forward positively. |

---

### 7. Manager track

For those in people management roles, the framework adds a parallel track covering:

- **People:** Supporting the growth and career development of direct reports. Handling sensitive issues. Coaching managers.
- **Team management:** Leading the strategic direction of squads. Covering for missing PM or DS Manager. Spanning multiple teams.
- **Communications:** Crafting messaging about discipline-wide initiatives. Ensuring teams understand and can move forward through change.
- **Strategy and planning:** Proactively identifying organisational change needs. Anticipating hiring needs. Building resilient, high-performing teams.

## Summary — why this framework works

**1. No overlap between seniority levels.** For each core competency, it is clear what is expected at each level. The assumption is that once you master the competencies of a level, you bring them with you to the next. This is why each level asks you to demonstrate *extra* responsibilities — not just the same ones again.

**2. No checkbox lists.** There is no mentioning of tooling or specialties throughout the framework. It doesn't matter if you do MLOps with Databricks or AWS. It doesn't matter if you've built 10 deep learning models if they don't add value. The framework focuses on *how* you apply your skills, not *which* tools you use.

**3. Easy to remember.** Six competencies (plus the manager track) is a number people can internalise. Team members can easily point out the core competencies they will be measured on — not the specific detail, but the themes. This helps frame any type of work, even in stand-ups or planning meetings.

**4. Detailed enough to guide individuals.** The long list of examples ensures there is little confusion between roles. Transparency is a feature, not a luxury.

**5. Flexible.** The levels presented might be most representative of the tech world, but flavours of these dimensions could be used to define a competency framework for a data science consultancy or agency too.
