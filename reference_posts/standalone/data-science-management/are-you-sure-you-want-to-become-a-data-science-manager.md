# Are you sure you want to become a Data Science Manager?

*Don't rush into the fancy title until you have read this.*

---

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Benjamin Elliott) | mood setter — opening image, person at a crossroads or pivotal moment]

Picture this. You have just delivered a killer project, your team's buzzing, and then — bam! You are asked, *"Have you ever thought about leading the team?"* Sounds tempting, right? But hold on — do you really know what you're signing up for?

As a Data Science manager, I have watched my teams grow from 0 to 12 data scientists and helped scale our DS discipline from 20 to 50+. I have also seen fellow managers leave for new challenges. Both instances created a vacuum to fill: the need of a manager to lead a data science squad. Filling this void can be an amazing opportunity, but I have also seen many colleagues fail to adjust to the transition.

**The transition to management is no joke.** Sure, it has its perks. But no one talks about the trade-offs. Most early managers walk in totally unprepared — that's where the frustration starts.

## First, tell me why

I always ask this question to potential people leads: *"Why do you want to become a manager?"*

Some common answers — and my challenge questions for each:

**Are you seeking recognition?** Moving into management comes with increased visibility — you'll lead decisions, interact with stakeholders, and guide strategy. But is this kind of recognition what excites you? And if so, what are you willing to trade in exchange for it?

**Are you stuck or bored?** If you've hit a plateau as an IC, the next step may seem like management. But before you jump in, consider: is this where your passion lies, or are you just looking for a way out of stagnation?

**Is it about the money?** In the tech space, managerial roles actually have the same salary ranges as their equivalent IC counterparts. Is it enough to trade in the technical work you love?

**Is it a requirement in your company?** In many organisations, career growth seems tied to management. But is management truly the only path to growth, or could you explore other ways to progress — even if it means moving to another company?

Notice the common denominator in all my challenge questions: **What is the trade-off?**

## Debunking the glorified "manager" title

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Museums Victoria) | analogy reinforcer — industrial era image representing the origins of "management"]

### It comes from the industrial revolution

The term "manager" has roots in the industrial era, when work was defined by efficiency, control, and supervision. In factories, managers oversaw production lines, ensured workers followed processes, and enforced order. Every time I hear the term "manager" referring to a people lead, I cringe.

### But the term is recognisable

Part of the reason we still use "manager" is simply because it's a catch-all term. It's familiar. In industries like data science, where innovation and collaboration are key, managers are more about facilitating success than enforcing authority. But the term persists because it's deeply embedded in corporate structures.

### I prefer the term "guide"

I have always disliked the term "manager" applied to people teams. My subconscious brain associates the word with authority and power, but in reality, you don't have that much decision-making power. A manager's job is to **guide and support your team**, removing roadblocks, and managing expectations from stakeholders. The "big" decisions often come from higher up, so in many cases, you become more of a facilitator than a direct decision-maker.

### Management is more lateral than vertical

In the tech space, becoming a manager is a **lateral move**. I have never seen a junior data scientist become a manager. It has always been those who reach senior data scientist level and have acquired enough technical knowledge who make the transition. In fact, it is a lateral move even on pay grade — many tech companies have the same salary bands for senior ICs and first-line managers.

## 5 things no one warns you about

Most people think being a manager means:
- Do 1-on-1s
- Some admin stuff
- Plan the weekly and quarterly work
- Interact with stakeholders on behalf of the team

These are true enough, but they only scratch the surface. These are **tasks, not responsibilities**. And responsibilities are what define what a manager's role really looks like.

### 1. Your project focus shifts from technical to strategic work

*You still need to be technical, but with a different focus.*

The temptation to roll up your sleeves and start coding is strong, especially when a project hits a roadblock or you know you can do the work 3 times faster. Reviewing pull requests or offering feedback on the structure of an analysis in a notebook? That's perfectly fine — these light-touch interventions allow you to stay engaged technically without bottlenecking the team. However, taking on a full data quality audit or single-handedly building a new model? That's too much.

What looked like a free coding week can easily become a crowded calendar, and suddenly there is no more time to write code and you are the blocker. When you do this, you're no longer leading your team — you're stepping back into the IC role.

But you **do** need to be technical enough to guide your team. A good manager understands the nuances of the work their team is doing. Your role has shifted from being the executor to the one who cultivates talent, raises standards, and ensures the team thrives.

### 2. Your impact is less clear

*What got you to success as an IC will not lead to success as a manager.*

As an IC, your success is directly tied to your output — your code, your models, your analyses. As a manager, your performance is evaluated by **how well your team performs**. This can be a tough adjustment for those used to being high performers.

**Stopping work is also part of success.** Recognising when to stop ongoing initiatives or not taking on too many projects is crucial. Spreading the team too thin can lead to long-term risks and create single points of failure.

**No performance drop in your absence is the ultimate goal.** Imagine you were to take a three-month leave, but your team maintains their output levels. That is a testament to how well you've set them up for success.

### 3. Your growth doesn't come from books

*Learning to lead takes experience, not just theory.*

As an IC, your learning can come from reading technical books and applying that knowledge directly to your work. As a manager, the real lessons come from **experience**. It is only when you have to tell someone that their performance is below what is expected, or when you have to influence the CEO to hire 10 more data scientists, that you will figure out how to do these things.

And there is no canvas, because everyone is different. Even these 2 scenarios will be experienced completely differently depending on the person in front of you. These situations require emotional intelligence, negotiation skills, and the ability to adapt on the fly.

### 4. Your task is to help others and the wider Data Science discipline grow

*It's not only about 1-on-1s.*

Are you willing to slow down immediate results to facilitate learning and growth for your team members? As a manager, you know which projects are coming in the next quarter and who is looking to move away from a project. This forces you to balance how people can be allocated to help them grow, whilst still delivering value.

**Are you willing to consider hiring as a first-class citizen?** Hiring is demanding on your time. You have to do your day-to-day job whilst allocating time for preparing questions, creating a standard scoring approach, and doing post-interview write-ups. 1 bad hire can make your life much worse than investing all this time upfront.

**Are you willing to go through restructuring?** As a manager, you are the bridge between your team and the company. Whether it's realigning team priorities, redefining roles, or living through layoffs, you play a critical role in ensuring that transitions are smooth and that the team comes out stronger.

### 5. You have to embrace conflict

*Because conflict is inevitable.*

**You can use some types of conflict for growth.** The geeky brainstorming or challenging other's solutions — everyone knows the goal and is generally happy to share ideas. As a manager, you need to create the environment where this "soft conflict" can happen.

**You can't please everyone.** There is also the "dark side of conflict" — conflicts between your direct report and yourself or between colleagues. In conflict situations, it's crucial to strike a balance between being empathetic and maintaining your authority. No one prepares you for these challenges.

**You can't shy away from conflict.** I know that interpersonal conflict is draining, but dealing with conflict is part of your job. In software engineering, when you run into bugs, you complain and moan, but you go and work it out. The same happens with conflict. If you don't take it seriously and deal with it head-on, conflict between people can become the reason for attrition, shifting priorities, and lower quality output. It is easy to say, *"dude, this is not my problem, go work it out yourself."* Bad news: like it or not, it is your problem too.

## Have you thought about the danger zone?

Before making the leap, there are 2 dimensions to consider.

### The battery filling exercise

Take a sheet of paper and make two columns:
- Column 1: Tasks and responsibilities that **fill your energy** — things you enjoy, that make you feel productive and fulfilled
- Column 2: Activities that **drain your energy**

Be brutally honest. The goal is to understand what drives your motivation. The goal as a manager is to have more days that fill your battery than drain it.

[Visual: diagram | author-generated | concept illustration — two-column battery filling/draining exercise]

### The premature leap assessment

**Management is not just about leading people — it's about leading technically proficient people.** If you haven't built a solid technical foundation first, jumping into management too early can leave you unable to guide your team through complex problems.

If you jump in too early, you can end up in what I call the **"danger zone."** Once you're in a managerial role, you're often bogged down with meetings, stakeholder management, and team leadership tasks, leaving little time to advance your own technical skills. You might find yourself in a frustrating limbo — you can't advance as a technical expert, but you also haven't fully mastered management.

What's worse, **going back to an IC role isn't always simple.** Companies may be hesitant to move you back, and you could face the challenge of rebuilding your technical credibility.

## However, becoming a manager opens many new and exciting opportunities

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Rowan Freeman) | mood setter — positive, open horizon image before the opportunities section]

Up until this point, things may seem daunting. But the rewards can be incredibly fulfilling if you lean into them.

- **A larger impact on business strategy.** As a manager, you are the one influencing what comes next. As an IC, you tend to be given a problem to solve — you don't have much to say about *which* problem to solve.

- **An opportunity to shape and cultivate talent.** You get to build and mentor a team. I have done this twice over, going from 0 data scientists to 6+ in each team. The feeling you get reaching these milestones is incredible.

- **A pseudo-PM role.** Your work as a manager aligns closely with product management. You never know if becoming a PM can be an option in the future.

- **A stronger network.** You tend to interact with senior leadership and cross-functional teams, allowing you to build a powerful network that can open doors for future career growth.

- **A unique skill set.** With fewer managers than ICs, you develop rare skills in leadership, people management, and process improvement. Your skill set is one of the highest valued — becoming proficient can help you throughout your career.

## Summary

Key takeaways before moving into Data Science management:

- **Reflect on your motivation.** Are you chasing recognition, stuck in a role, or feeling pressured? Be clear on why you're considering management, and weigh the trade-offs.
- **"Manager" is not a promotion.** In tech, transitioning to management is typically a lateral move. It comes with new responsibilities but not necessarily more power or pay.
- **Management shifts your focus.** Your role moves from hands-on technical work to guiding strategy, mentoring, and removing roadblocks.
- **Success isn't about you anymore.** It's about the team. Your performance is measured by how well others perform.
- **Prepare for conflict.** Conflict is inevitable — both the constructive, idea-driven kind and the challenging interpersonal issues. Learn to embrace and navigate both.
- **Mind the danger zone.** Jumping into management too early can trap you in a space where you can't advance technically, yet you're not experienced enough as a leader.
- **New opportunities await.** From influencing business strategy and building strong networks to potentially transitioning into product management, management can open doors you hadn't considered.
