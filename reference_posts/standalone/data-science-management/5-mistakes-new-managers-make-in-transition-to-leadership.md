# 5 mistakes I see new managers make in their transition into leadership roles

*Break free from the individual contributor mindset. You are a manager now.*

---

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Jose Aragones) | mood setter — opening image, person at the start of a new path]

You have been working for years as a data scientist, going from junior to senior. Over the course of these years, you have a great delivery track record, you tend to solve the biggest problems and you are a trusted member of the Data Science discipline when it comes to technical solutions. There is an open position to lead one of the Data Science teams, and the business has identified you as a candidate to lead it.

You feel it is an exciting opportunity! You have actually been reading a lot about management lately and believe it can be an area you can grow in. Not only that, but you have been lucky to work for 1 or 2 great standout managers, who have become inspirations for the leader you hope to become. You even feel you understand some of the trade-offs this transition will require.

Yet, despite all the preparation, no amount of reading or inspiration can fully convey what it feels like to move from individual contributor to manager. It's like learning to drive: you may have watched your parents or friends drive for years, and you may know what all the signs mean, but once you're at the wheel, it is a complete new world.

In this blog post, I will cover 5 areas where new data science managers often stumble during their transition from IC to leadership. My goal is to:

1. Highlight "Stop doing" scenarios
2. Explain why these stop doing scenarios can be a pitfall for you and the team
3. Suggest an alternative "Start doing" approach that better aligns with effective management

I hope this post helps you with a clear checklist of do vs not-do's for your first few months in your new manager role. As I said before, your first few driving trips are stressful, but with the right tips, driving will become natural. Let's hit the road!

---

## Before getting started: team size matters, but not that much

There is no denying that being a manager of 2 and working with 1 or 2 projects is not a massive shift to what you were doing as a senior data scientist. You probably just need to account for a bit of extra time for 1-on-1s, do some admin stuff, focus more on planning the weekly and quarterly work and have a bit more interaction with stakeholders on behalf of the team.

However, I have seen many of these mistakes happening even in small teams. Think of the mentality shift as climbing a mountain. Early on, you can carry all your gear and keep going on your own. But as you reach higher altitudes, you need a team, and a strategy for helping each other up. If you keep climbing solo, you'll eventually reach a ledge you can't pass. Shifting to a management mindset allows you to set up ropes, delegate tasks, and work as a team to reach new heights together.

## 1st STOP: Stop coding

When you are transitioning to a manager role, it is tempting to hold onto the technical work that brought you success as an IC. After all, you built those models, fine-tuned the ETLs and achieved amazing results. You know you can accomplish certain tasks 50% faster than your direct reports, and you likely feel that your contributions will be more impactful if you continue to "jump in and do the work" yourself. But here's the reality: **coding can quickly become a trap that prevents you from fully stepping into your new role.**

[Visual: illustration | Dall-E | concept image — the trap of staying in IC mode as a new manager]

Even in the best case scenario, where you can actually take on all your new management tasks plus what you did as an IC, you are overlooking the long-term impacts. Here's why the coding zone works against you and your team:

1. **By definition, you have less time than before.** Even tasks that feel quick (1-on-1s, admin) add up. Either you work more hours, or you cut corners on quality. You simply have less time than when you were an IC.
2. **Creating an unnecessary dependency.** If you continue to code with less and less time for it, you might suddenly become the team's bottleneck. If you are the only one who knows how to build and maintain a PyTorch model, what happens when there is an error at 2am?
3. **Stunting team growth.** When you keep doing the work yourself, you inadvertently prevent your team members from taking on challenges in areas they haven't explored.
4. **Missing the big picture.** The more time you spend in the technical zone, the less time you spend understanding how the team's work aligns with company objectives.

**What to start doing?** Delegate meaningfully. Start with outcome-based assignments rather than prescriptive tasks. Trust your team to find the solution, and position yourself as the person who removes blockers rather than the one solving every problem.

---

## 2nd STOP: Stop solving all the problems yourself

I can tell you from experience that this is achievable. In Spain, we have 5 months paternity leave plus holidays. I literally disappeared for a good chunk of that time. When I returned, I found that, aside from a few strategic decisions, everything had run smoothly. Delivery was on track, and targets were met. **Honestly, that should be the goal of every team lead.**

**What to start doing?**

1. **Start with the destination in mind.** When your team encounters a challenge, describe the desired outcome rather than giving step-by-step instructions. For example, *"We want to increase model accuracy by next quarter. What approaches would you suggest?"* or *"Wouldn't it be amazing if we were able to predict competitor's prices? I know this is difficult, but what could we try?"* This sets clear goals and encourages creative thinking.

2. **Delegate ownership, not just tasks.** Ownership takes many forms:
   - *Junior team members* could own facilitating reading groups, running retro sessions, or handling alerting and monitoring. These build confidence.
   - *Mid-level contributors* might own a subcomponent of a larger project — model validation, data monitoring, or implementing a testing framework.
   - *Senior members* could lead entire project streams or direct collaborations with other departments.

---

## 3rd STOP: Stop trying to be the best at everything

As a manager, it's tempting to want to stay on top of every technical skill. After all, you've built your career as an IC by mastering complex problems. But in my experience, trying to keep up with everything is no longer sustainable.

1. **Your time is more limited now.** Even with a positive mindset — wanting to learn to help your team — you'll soon find that you're spreading yourself too thin.
2. **Being the best requires getting hands-on, not just reading books.** Sure, you can read 10 O'Reilly books, but until you apply what you read, you will not master concepts. And you don't have the time for that anymore.

**What to start doing?**

1. **Prioritise quality over perfection.** Let go of the need to know every technical detail. Your value comes from enabling quality through review processes, peer feedback, and clear expectations — not from being the sole quality enforcer.
2. **Learn from your team.** Shift from learning everything yourself to helping your team members learn. If someone wants to dive into recommender systems, support them by providing resources and connecting them with experts. Then get them to share that knowledge back to the team.

## 4th STOP: Stop relying on technical expertise for value

You need to be very careful that technical expertise — coding, problem-solving, theoretical knowledge — doesn't become your comfort zone and safe space. As a manager, continuing to derive your primary value from technical skills alone is **like trying to win a chess match using only pawns** — you're limiting your potential impact.

**What to start doing?**

1. **Enhance your communication toolkit.** As a manager, the number of presentations you lead increases exponentially. Learn to adjust your style for different audiences. Understand when Confluence is more effective than PowerPoint and vice versa.
2. **Be comfortable boasting about team wins.** Yes, you read that right. Build your own and your team's visibility. Learn to translate technical achievements into business value. If you don't talk about these, even though you might be adding a lot of value, only a few people will know about it.
3. **Start playing Littlefinger** *(from Game of Thrones)*. Build relationships with stakeholders — don't forget about non-technical departments. Your influence as a manager extends far beyond your immediate team.

---

## 5th STOP: Stop assuming your team knows the full context

One of the most common — and damaging — assumptions a new manager makes is that the team has the same information they do. They don't.

**What to start doing?**

1. **Over-communicate the "why."** When priorities change or decisions are made above your level, don't just pass down the "what." Explain how it aligns with broader business objectives or recent market changes.
2. **In 1-on-1s, explicitly check for understanding.** *"How clear is our team's priority shift to you? What questions do you have about why we made this change?"* Sometimes the team doesn't want to ask these questions in an open forum, but 1-on-1s are a great space to open that circle of trust.
3. **When priorities shift, walk through the full context.** For example, once we had a full quarter planned, but mid-quarter we were told there was a big gap in our Hotels vertical target metrics. Whilst we were focusing on Flights, the decision was made to shift resources. Walking the team through the full picture — not just the outcome — builds trust and alignment.

## Summary

[Visual: photo | Unsplash (Meizhi Lang) | section separator — closing image before summary]

You might be thinking "well, all of this is easier said than done." True. But if you don't stop doing, I can guarantee your role as a manager will not be meeting expectations in the long run.

Remember the learning to drive analogy? Apply it to your growth as a manager: be aware of what you are not comfortable with and keep practising. Do it step by step.

- **1st STOP:** Stop coding
- **2nd STOP:** Stop solving all the problems yourself
- **3rd STOP:** Stop trying to be the best at everything
- **4th STOP:** Stop relying on technical expertise for value
- **5th STOP:** Stop assuming your team knows the full context

Keep the 5 STOPs in mind, and you will see a positive change over time.
