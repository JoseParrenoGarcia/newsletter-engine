# Struggling to be a great tech manager? This book has answers for you

*Lessons from "The Manager's Path" that every engineer-turned-manager should know.*

---

Before I talk about "The Manager's Path", I have a mini-story to tell you. I promise to be brief.

In 2022, I was given the opportunity to build a Data Science marketing team from scratch, which was no small ask. Data Science hadn't had a team working under the marketing umbrella before. One moment, I was a successful individual contributor; the next, I was faced with defining a role that hadn't existed. In fact, I wasn't told why they had chosen me for the role, so it was really confusing to understand what was expected from me. Camille's chapter on "Becoming a Tech Lead" helped me answer these questions and calm my nerves. It reminded me to focus on the strengths that had earned me the opportunity. The end of the story is that not only did I build the team but also scaled it to 8 data scientists.

Of course, my experience wasn't shaped by this 1 chapter in the book. But it's not easy to recall specifics from a 200-page guide. Whilst the book has 9 sections, 6 were very relevant to me. I want to capture key phrases and lessons that stuck with me, so you can benefit from them as I have.

The 6 sections:
1. Mentorship — a great first step to demonstrate leadership
2. Being a tech lead — leading without formal authority
3. Managing People — from leading projects to managing individuals
4. Managing a team — it's not simply about having more 1-on-1s
5. Managing multiple teams — delegation is your new superpower
6. Managing managers — helping others lead

I strongly recommend those of you just stepping into management or looking to refine your leadership skills to read this book.

## 1. Mentorship — a great first step to leading

> *"The first act of people management for many engineers is often unofficial."* [page 11]

Mentoring someone should bring you out of your comfort zone. It's probably the first time you have *"the feeling of being responsible for another person."* [page 12]

There are 3 types of mentorship:

**Mentoring a new hire.** Prepare good documentation on technical setups. Spend time updating these documents if they encounter surprises. Introduce this person not only to your team, but to those you work closely with. Make sure they are comfortable meeting with leadership. Your small time investment can mean the world for a new person on the team.

**Mentoring a colleague on career progressions.** Your success as a mentor depends on your ability to listen. Don't provide straight answers. Try to understand why your mentee is asking certain questions. Establish what "done" means for the mentorship.

**Offering yourself as a mentor.** If you see someone struggling, bring up the possibility of having a few 1-on-1 sessions with them. Mentoring doesn't have to begin with a formal sit-down. But mentoring is a first taste of what it means to help someone altruistically.

## 2. Being a tech lead — leading without formal authority

> *"All great tech leads know this one weird trick: the willingness to step away from the code."* [page 31]

I really like this phrase and I have highlighted "willingness" on purpose. A tech lead will probably write code, but she knows when not coding is the right thing to do.

What does a great tech lead do?

1. A tech lead should not have management responsibilities. They might have 1-on-1s with their peers, but probably to cover project details.
2. A tech lead should be senior enough to be provided the toughest problems the team is facing.
3. A good tech lead is a multiplier, making everyone on the team more effective.

**One issue that tech leads face:** Tech leads are positioned to act as strong technical project leaders, but they have no formal authority. They are not the team manager, nor the product owner. Tech leads don't tend to be in management meetings, so they might miss headcount discussions. They also don't tend to be in product long-term discussions, so they might miss if their current projects are still high priority.

Therefore, if as a tech lead you are able to influence your manager and your current product owner counterparts, this is an indication of great leadership skills.

## 3. Managing People — from leading projects to managing individuals

> *"It's hard to accept that 'new manager' is an entry-level job with no seniority on any front, but that's the best mindset with which to start leading."* [page 49]

Managing people is a whole new game compared to being a tech lead. This is the very first time where you feel that your coding time decreases at an alarming rate, and it is replaced with other tasks.

### Build trust and rapport

Regardless of whether you are a people person, there are actions which can bring you closer to your direct reports. Have periodic 1-on-1s (these can be a simple catchup, a feedback meeting, or a project progress report). Make sure people participate and feel like they are part of discussions. If someone is not accustomed to dealing with new responsibilities, be there with them in their first few meetings. **People perform best when they feel safe.**

### 2 styles to avoid

**Micromanagement:** *"Micromanagement creeps up on you. If micromanagement is your default approach toward leading your team, you'll end up accidentally undermining the very people you need to be growing and rewarding. Trust and control are the main issues around micromanagement."* [page 58]

**Extreme delegation:** *"Delegation is not the same thing as abdication. When you're delegating responsibility, you're still expected to be involved as much as is necessary to help the project succeed."* [page 58]

### Performance reviews are not a simple admin task

Some managers don't understand how important performance reviews are. As an IC, you expect a detailed performance summary from your manager. Now as a manager, you have to provide this detailed view to all your direct reports. Here are guidelines from the book:

1. **Give yourself enough time, and start early.** *"This process isn't something you can knock out in an hour and do well."* [page 64] Start collecting feedback throughout the year.
2. **Try to account for the whole year, not just the past couple of months.** The goal is to recognise not just early accomplishments but the full arc of someone's growth.
3. Never surprise someone at a performance review. It's not fair for someone to be told their bonus is 50% instead of 100% because they underperformed, when it's the first time they hear about this.

## 4. Managing a team — it's not simply about having more 1-on-1s

> *"Managing a team is more than just doing the job of managing the individuals."* [page 75]

When a team increases headcount, dynamics become increasingly complex. You can't cover all possible issues just through 1-on-1s.

### Staying technical

> *"If you truly wish to command the respect of an engineering team, they must see you as technically credible."* [page 77]

It doesn't mean you have to be the best engineer. But who wants to explain technical concepts to a manager they believe doesn't understand the problems they face? How are you going to guide technical decisions if you don't truly follow the tech? You shouldn't suddenly stop coding at all — help in peer reviews, pick up smaller "boring" or well-defined tasks.

### Shielding the team — give context, not just orders

If you don't provide the team with context on why priorities are changing mid-quarter, they might see it as an attack on their work. But if they understand how important the new priority is, they might close ranks and all rally to help.

### Team cohesion — the brilliant jerk

Camille provides stereotypes of engineers who can break team cohesion. The one I've seen most often is **the brilliant jerk** — an amazing engineer, but ego-driven, who can create a toxic environment that undermines team morale and collaboration.

## 5. Managing multiple teams — delegation is your new superpower

> *"It's time — now — for you to figure out how to manage your time."* [page 103]

Multiple-team management is a completely new job. The book offers 2 simple prioritisation matrices:

**Which tasks really require your attention?** Understand the difference between importance and urgency. [Eisenhower-style matrix applied to management tasks]

[Visual: diagram | author-generated (book screenshot) | framework illustration — importance vs urgency 2x2 matrix for managing time]

**Which tasks really need you specifically to complete them?**

> *"Delegation is the primary way you claw yourself out of the feeling of having too many plates spinning at once."* [page 108]

### Skip-level meetings

Camille talks about skip-level meetings as a way to understand team health:

> *"If you want to build a strong management team, understanding the people who report to those managers and maintaining a relationship with them is hard to avoid."* [page 128]

Information gets lost between layers — not because managers are hiding anything, but because subtle details fade in the relay. Useful skip-level questions from the book:

- What do you like best/worst about the project you are working on?
- What's keeping you from doing your best work right now?
- Are there any opportunities you think we might be missing?
- Are there any areas of the business strategy you don't understand?
- What could we do to make working at the company more fun?
- What can I provide for you and the team?

[Visual: illustration | Dall-E | concept image — managing at scale and skip-level relationships]

### Manager accountability

Managers should make your life easier. Even in tough scenarios, your manager should be held responsible. As a manager of managers, your job is to help them identify problems and find the solutions or support the path forward:

> *"They've done the hard work of identifying the problems that are slowing down their teams, but you need to then help find the solutions or support the path forward."* [page 132]

## 6. Managing managers — helping others lead

Managing new managers has a clearer playbook than managing experienced managers.

**New managers need coaching on:**
- Not being overworked — helping them hand off old IC responsibilities
- Running effective 1-on-1s and providing structured feedback
- Stakeholder communications (often completely forgotten in the rush to manage)

> *"It's no surprise that first-time managers need a lot of coaching."* [page 136]

**Helping experienced managers is less tangible.** They probably make your life easy, but be wary of the sub-culture they might introduce, and the blind spots that can develop when someone is left unsupported.

**When hiring managers, look for:**

1. How they talk about their previous teams — do they take credit, or spread it?
2. How they handle conflict — can they manage both the "geeky" and "interpersonal" kinds?
3. How they communicate up, down, and sideways
4. How they balance staying hands-on vs delegating
5. Technical credibility — Camille proposes mediating a mock technical dispute rather than a coding interview
6. Cultural fit — the most important and the hardest to interview for

> *"Ask the references to describe the ways that the person succeeds as well as the ways she fails. Ask them if they would work with or for this person again. Ask them what they love about the person, and what drives them crazy."* [page 143]

## Summary

> *"This book is for engineering managers. It's not a generic management book."* [page 77]

In the ever-evolving journey of leadership, *The Manager's Path* offers a guide for engineers navigating the transition from mentoring to managing teams and beyond. From taking your first steps as a mentor to leading multiple teams, Camille Fournier reminds us that **leadership is not about having all the answers** — it's about empowering others, making hard decisions, and continuously refining our craft. Her insights are not abstract. She always provides tangible examples to refer to.

Whether you're stepping into management for the first time or guiding others on their leadership path, the knowledge in these pages is timeless. Let it inspire you to lead not only with skill but with heart.
