# Post Type: Management

For posts about leadership, team dynamics, career transitions, EQ, feedback, meetings, recognition, and org design. These are Jose's most personal posts — grounded in lived experience and written for data scientists navigating people management.

Load alongside `shared/voice.md` and `shared/anti_patterns.md`.

---

## Opening Pattern

**Personal anecdote → framing → thesis.**

The opening paragraph is always grounded in a real, specific situation — not a hypothetical. It could be Jose's own experience (commute, paternity leave, a difficult conversation), a friend's or colleague's situation named by role ("a friend of mine who became a manager..."), or a vivid scene that readers will recognise.

The anecdote establishes emotional credibility before any framework or advice. Once the scene is set, the inquiry is framed — either through a **rhetorical question** ("But how do you really know if you trust your manager?") or a **direct thesis statement** ("I restructured my week around three principles"). The rhetorical question is more common in reflective/exploratory posts; the direct statement in tactical/framework posts.

**Corpus examples:**
- *"I live far from the office, and meeting the required work-from-the-office policy was tough (family health and new born kids were the main reasons)... But how do you really know if you trust your manager?"* — anecdote → rhetorical question
- *"Leading 2 Data Science squads... I restructured my week around the 3Ps"* — anecdote → direct thesis
- *"A few years ago, a friend of mine told me about her manager..."*

**What to avoid:** Opening with a definition, a statistic, or a generic preamble. Management posts earn the reader's attention through story first.

---

## Section Structure

- **5–8 thematic sections** per post
- Headings are **noun-phrase declarations**, not questions or imperatives: "Trust: the backbone of a solid relationship", "Ownership: where leadership begins", "Emotional intelligence: the skill no one taught you"
- Each section contains **3–5 prose paragraphs** — no bullet walls
- Sections *sometimes* include a **"Take a moment to reflect"** prompt at the end — a single question or short set of questions inviting the reader to apply the concept to their own situation. This is optional and appears more in reflective posts than in tactical/framework posts.
- Sections may end with a **named blockquote** from an external authority (Goleman, Kahneman, Camille Fournier, etc.) — never an anonymous quote

**Section length rhythm:** The opening and closing sections tend to be shorter (scene-setting and synthesis). Middle sections — where the frameworks live — are the densest.

---

## Closing Pattern

**No hard CTA. Ends with reflection or synthesis.**

The closing invites the reader to assess their own situation or leaves them with a lasting thought — not a subscription push, not an urgent call to action.

Two valid closing patterns:

1. **Open reflective question** — used in exploratory/reflective posts. Final line is a question.
   - *"Does your manager embody these traits? If so, take a moment to appreciate them. If not, consider what steps you can take to start an open dialogue."*
   - *"What traits have you learned to value over time?"*

2. **Summary with reflection** — used in tactical/framework posts. Recaps the framework, then ends with a forward-looking observation.
   - *"The 3Ps are not a perfect system. Time will tell if it works long-term — but my calendar is mine again."*

Both patterns feel like the end of a thoughtful conversation, not a sales pitch.

## Formatting

| Element | Rule |
|---------|------|
| `##` headings | Use for main sections. Noun-phrase declarations. |
| `###` headings | Use sparingly for sub-sections within a complex section. |
| **Bold** | Key concept names and sub-themes within prose. Not for section titles. |
| `> Blockquote` | Named external attribution only. Author name on the same line or next. |
| Bullet lists | Frameworks and taxonomies (5 EQ skills, 3 feedback dimensions, 7 meeting types). Not for general prose. |
| Visual placeholders | Photos as section separators. Add `[Visual: photo | Dall-E | section separator — description]` between major sections. |
| Emoji | None. |

---

## Tone

- **Warm, peer-level, self-disclosing.** Jose is not above the reader — he has made these mistakes, learned these lessons.
- **Present tense for general principles.** "Great managers earn trust over time." Not "Great managers earned trust."
- **Challenges reader assumptions** (myth-busting) or **invites self-assessment** — never lectures.
- **Occasionally self-deprecating or humorous**, but grounded. Cultural references are welcome (Game of Thrones, football, personal family life) when they fit naturally — never forced.
